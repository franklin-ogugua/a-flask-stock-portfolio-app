Stock Analysis Guide

Overview

This guide provides an overview into how to analyze stocks, including the stock metrics that are displayed in the watchlist.

Investing Philosophy

Investing in stocks in difficult, but it is a great long-term strategy for accumulating wealth.

Stocks have historically outperformed other investments, such as bonds, commodities, CDs (Certificates of Deposit), savings accounts, etc.

When investing in stocks, there are two different approaches: passive and active.

Passive investing means buying broad-market index funds or ETFs (Exchange Traded Funds) and holding on to them for the long-term (5+ years). For example:

  • Vanguard S&P 500 Index Fund
  • Vanguard S&P 500 ETF
  • Invesco QQQ (tracks Nasdaq 100)

Active investing means taking a more hands-on approach by picking individual stocks to purchase and trading at a reasonable frequency (not day-trading!).

Combining both approaches is actually a great tool, as some of your assets are tracking major indices (passive investments), while you are also keeping track of individual stocks to purchase/sell based on market conditions and trends.

Stock Analysis - Key Considerations

Determining which stocks to purchase (and subsequently when to sell them) is an art, not a science. Unfortunately, there is no clear approach that always works or everyone would make tons of money in the stock market.

However, there are a lot of key areas to focus on when selecting stocks that can help in picking successful stock investments:

  • Financial conditions - do the key financial metrics show a healthy company relative to its stock price?
  • Economic moat - does the company have a competitive advantage?
  • Great brand - is there intrinsic value in the brand that adds value to the stock?
  • Growth opportunities - are there opportunities in the near future for the company to grow?

Stock Analysis - Financial Metrics

52-Week High/Low

The 52-week high refers to the highest market price of a given security over a 52-week (one year) period.

The 52-week high serves as an indicator for potential investors. Investor's will often reference the 52-week high for a stock when looking at the current price.

While it may be tempting to think that you should buy a stock when it's close to it's 52-week low and sell when it's close to its 52-week high, this approach isn't guaranteed to work.

In fact, a lot of great stocks will trade near there 52-week high as their stock price continues to rise.

Market Capitalization

Market capitalization (or market cap) is the total value of the tradable shares of a publicly traded company; it is equal to the share price times the number of shares outstanding. As outstanding stock is bought and sold in public markets, capitalization could be used as a proxy for the public opinion of a company's net worth and is a determining factor in some forms of stock valuation. Preferred shares are included in the calculation.

Rule of thumb for different cap classes:

  • Mega-cap: Over $200 billion
  • Large-cap: Over $10 billion
  • Mid-cap: $2 billion–$10 billion
  • Small-cap: $250 million–$2 billion
  • Micro-cap: Below $250 million
  • Nano-cap: Below $50 million

Dividends

Dividends are payments that a company makes to its shareholders (people that own its stock).

Typically, larger and well-established companies will pay out dividends.

There is a sub-category of investing that focuses entirely on investing in dividend-paying stocks for long-term investing.

A quick metric to check for a company is the "Dividend Per Share", which defines how much money the company pays each shareholder over a 1-year period.

Price-to-Earnings (PE) Ratio

It's not unusual to pay a P/E of as much as 20 times earnings for a faster-growing company with a strong competitive advantage, but make sure the price you pay isn't too high for the quality of company you're buying.

The S&P 500 has historically traded for around 16 times earnings. So a struggling-yet-recovering company might be a bargain with a high single-digit P/E, whereas a stronger business could be bargain with a P/E in the high teens.

Price-to-Earnings-Growth (PEG) Ratio

The PEG Ratio takes the P/E Ratio and incorporates the expected earnings growth to provide a valuation of a company.

At a high-level, a PEG Ratio can be interpreted as:

  • < 1.0 - indicates that a stock is undervalued
  • > 1.0 - indicates a stock is overvalued

However, the PEG ratio can vary from industry to industry and it fluctuates based on overall market conditions as well.

It's best to use the PEG ratio when comparing companies in similar industries (ie. Home Depot vs. Lowes)

More information: Motley Fool - What Is the PEG Ratio?

Profit Margin

Profit margin indicates the percentage of sales that get turned into profits. This metric gauges how well a company is making money.

This metric quickly indicates if a company is profitable or not. As with the PEG Ratio, profit margins vary by industry.

Price-to-Book Ratio

The book value of a company is the value of its assets minus its liabilities (which can be found in a company's balance sheet).

One of the key concepts of value investing is finding stocks that are under-priced. The book value of a company is often used to find under-priced stocks.

The Price-to-Book Value ratio is a good metric for comparing companies in similar industries.
